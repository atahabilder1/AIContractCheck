\subsection{RQ1: Vulnerability Prevalence}

\textbf{Finding: 64.5\% of compilable LLM-generated contracts contain at least one vulnerability.}

Across 2,400 generated contracts, 1,566 (65.2\%) compiled successfully. Our analysis considers only compilable contracts; contracts that fail compilation represent a separate quality dimension analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}. On the compiled subset, our pipeline detected a total of 5,134 vulnerabilities after cross-tool deduplication. Table~\ref{tab:overall} summarizes the overall prevalence by severity.

On average, each compiled contract contains 3.28 vulnerabilities (SD = 4.48), with a median of 2 and a heavily right-skewed distribution, with a long tail of contracts containing 10+ vulnerabilities, predominantly in complex categories such as DeFi staking and auctions.

\textbf{Compilation rates vary dramatically across LLMs}, representing a critical and previously unreported quality dimension. GPT-5 achieves the highest compilation rate (91.0\%), followed closely by GPT-4o (90.0\%), while CodeLlama compiles only 34.7\% of contracts (Fig.~\ref{fig:compilation} in Appendix). This means that CodeLlama's apparently low vulnerability counts in raw analyses are an artifact of compilation failure, not superior security. When analyzing only compiled contracts, the vulnerability landscape changes substantially.

26.8\% of compiled contracts contain at least one \emph{high-severity} vulnerability---one that could lead to direct financial loss if exploited. This is particularly concerning given that developers may deploy these contracts with minimal review~\cite{perry2023users}.

\textbf{Raw vulnerability counts are confounded by contract size.} Claude generates the largest contracts (mean 113 LOC), which partly explains its highest raw vulnerability count (mean 7.64). When normalized by lines of code, the picture changes substantially: Gemini exhibits the highest vulnerability density (8.43 vulns/100 LOC), followed by DeepSeek (7.96), Claude (6.98), Qwen (6.95), Codestral (6.88), GPT-4o (6.61), CodeLlama (5.16), and GPT-5 (2.22). A Kruskal-Wallis test on LOC-normalized rates remains significant ($H = 172.2$, $p < 10^{-34}$), confirming that cross-LLM differences persist even after controlling for contract size. Contract size correlates moderately with vulnerability count (Spearman $\rho = 0.506$, $p < 10^{-102}$).

GPT-5 stands out as an outlier: it combines the highest compilation rate (91.0\%) with the lowest vulnerability density (2.22 vulns/100 LOC), suggesting that newer-generation models may incorporate improved security awareness. However, even GPT-5 produces contracts where 35.5\% have at least one vulnerability.

The effect size between Claude and CodeLlama is large ($\delta = 0.64$), but this comparison must be interpreted carefully: CodeLlama compiles only 34.7\% of contracts, meaning its ``safer'' compiled subset represents only its simplest outputs. \subsubsection{General-Purpose vs.\ Code-Specialized}
Grouping models by type reveals that general-purpose LLMs compile more frequently (74.8\% vs.\ 55.8\%) and produce more total vulnerabilities per contract (mean 3.96 vs.\ 2.36, $U = 339{,}493$, $p < 10^{-5}$). However, when normalized by lines of code, the difference vanishes entirely: 6.66 vs.\ 6.97 vulns/100 LOC ($p = 0.094$, Cliff's $\delta = 0.05$, negligible). The raw vulnerability gap is explained by general-purpose models producing larger contracts (65.3 vs.\ 34.9 mean LOC). High-severity rates are virtually identical (26.9\% vs.\ 26.6\%). Thus, the General/Code distinction does not predict vulnerability density---model-to-model variation dominates.

\subsubsection{Tool Agreement Analysis}
Our multi-tool approach is essential: Slither dominates detection (4,342 total findings), followed by Semgrep (1,296) and Mythril (262). The low inter-tool agreement (Spearman correlations: Slither--Semgrep $\rho = 0.307$, Slither--Mythril $\rho = 0.186$, Mythril--Semgrep $\rho = 0.141$; Fig.~\ref{fig:tool_agreement} in Appendix) confirms that no single tool provides adequate coverage, validating our multi-tool pipeline design.

\subsection{RQ4: Adversarial Prompt Effectiveness}

\textbf{Finding: Adversarial prompts do NOT increase vulnerability rates---the effect is negligible.}

This is our most surprising and theoretically significant result. Contrary to expectations based on prior adversarial prompt research~\cite{kang2024exploiting}, adversarial prompts show no significant effect on vulnerability rates. Table~\ref{tab:adversarial} summarizes the results.

The overall effect is not statistically significant ($p = 0.997$, Cliff's $\delta = -0.090$, negligible effect). Standard prompts yield numerically more vulnerabilities, but this is fully explained by code complexity: standard prompts produce larger contracts (mean 59.6 LOC vs.\ 49.6 LOC). The direction is opposite to what adversarial prompt literature would predict, yet the effect size is negligible.

Per-LLM analysis reveals no consistent pattern: adversarial prompts produce marginally fewer vulnerabilities for most LLMs (Claude: 6.79 vs.\ 10.22; GPT-4o: 2.59 vs.\ 4.08; Qwen: 2.33 vs.\ 3.85), but marginally more for GPT-5 (1.92 vs.\ 1.53). No individual LLM shows a significant adversarial effect after Bonferroni correction.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../visualization/figures/fig2_standard_vs_adversarial.pdf}
    \caption{Vulnerability count by LLM for standard vs.\ adversarial prompts. (a) Per-LLM mean vulnerabilities show standard prompts produce slightly more across most models. (b) Overall distributions are nearly identical ($p = 0.997$, Cliff's $\delta = -0.090$), supporting the intrinsic vulnerability hypothesis.}
    \label{fig:adversarial_bar}

The key explanatory mechanism is \textbf{code complexity}: adversarial prompts produce smaller contracts (mean 49.6 LOC vs.\ 59.6 LOC). Prompts emphasizing ``simplicity'' or ``minimal implementation'' cause LLMs to generate less code, which mechanically contains fewer opportunities for vulnerabilities. The ``no dependencies'' trigger, which we hypothesized would increase vulnerability rates by forcing manual reimplementation of library code, instead produces smaller contracts that compile less frequently.

The overall difference across adversarial trigger categories is not significant (Kruskal-Wallis $H = 5.04$, $p = 0.656$; Fig.~\ref{fig:adv_triggers} in Appendix), indicating that no particular trigger category is more effective at inducing vulnerabilities than others.

\textbf{Implications.} This null adversarial result is arguably more concerning than a positive result would be. It implies that \emph{vulnerabilities are intrinsic to LLM code generation}, arising from patterns in training data rather than from prompt manipulation. Developers cannot ``prompt their way'' to secure code---even well-intentioned, detailed prompts produce vulnerable contracts. This shifts the mitigation burden from prompt engineering to post-generation tooling and model-level improvements.

\subsection{RQ5: LLM-Specific Vulnerability Patterns}

\textbf{Finding: LLMs exhibit distinct vulnerability fingerprints not seen in human code.}

We identify three LLM-specific patterns:

\subsubsection{Phantom Guard Pattern}
LLMs generate modifier declarations (e.g., \texttt{nonReentrant}) that are syntactically present but functionally incomplete---the modifier body contains no state-locking logic. Fig.~\ref{fig:phantom} illustrates this pattern. Systematic analysis across all 2,400 contracts identified 1 instance out of 1,637 total modifiers (0.06\%), occurring in a Gemini-generated contract. While rare, this pattern is particularly insidious because code review may miss it---the modifier \emph{name} suggests protection, but the implementation provides none.

\begin{figure}[t]
\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Phantom Guard Pattern: The \texttt{nonReentrant} modifier is declared but contains no locking logic (left). A correct implementation requires a state variable and checks (right).}, label={fig:phantom}, language=Solidity, basicstyle=\scriptsize\ttfamily]
// LLM-generated (VULNERABLE)
modifier nonReentrant() {
    _; // No lock! Just executes body
}

function withdraw(uint amt) nonReentrant {
    payable(msg.sender).call{value: amt}("");
    balances[msg.sender] -= amt; // State
}                                // after call

// Correct implementation (OpenZeppelin)
uint256 private _status;
modifier nonReentrant() {
    require(_status != 2, "Reentrant");
    _status = 2;
    _;
    _status = 1;
}
\end{lstlisting}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Inherited Vulnerability Pattern}
When prompted to avoid dependencies, LLMs attempt manual reimplementations of OpenZeppelin patterns that contain subtle bugs. Systematic analysis found 91 out of 2,258 contracts targeting Solidity $\geq$0.8.0 (4.0\%) unnecessarily include SafeMath. This is most prevalent in DeepSeek (9.7\%), Gemini (8.7\%), and Codestral (8.3\%), while Claude and GPT-5 never exhibit this pattern. Paradoxically, code-specialized LLMs are more likely to include unnecessary SafeMath than general-purpose models. Fig.~\ref{fig:inherited} shows a representative example.

\begin{figure}[t]
\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Inherited Vulnerability: LLM-generated SafeMath reimplementation on Solidity 0.8+, where built-in overflow checks make it unnecessary and the manual implementation may introduce subtle bugs.}, label={fig:inherited}, language=Solidity, basicstyle=\scriptsize\ttfamily]
// LLM-generated SafeMath (UNNECESSARY on 0.8+)
function safeAdd(uint a, uint b)
    internal pure returns (uint) {
    uint c = a + b;
    require(c >= a); // Built-in since 0.8
    return c;
}

// LLM-generated Ownable (VULNERABLE)
function transferOwnership(address new)
    public onlyOwner {
    owner = new; // No zero-address check!
}               // Owner can be lost forever
\end{lstlisting}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Context Window Degradation}
Of 2,400 contracts, 543 (22.6\%) exceed 100 lines. In these longer contracts, risky code patterns (external calls, assembly, unchecked blocks) appear at 4.13$\times$ higher density in the second half compared to the first half (1.24 vs.\ 0.30 per 100 LOC), with 46.2\% of contracts exhibiting this degradation pattern. The effect varies dramatically by LLM: DeepSeek shows the strongest degradation (14.1$\times$), followed by Gemini (8.4$\times$) and GPT-4o (5.1$\times$), while Claude (3.2$\times$) and CodeLlama (3.1$\times$) show the least. This degradation is statistically significant (Spearman $\rho = 0.270$ between degradation ratio and vulnerability count, $p < 0.001$), suggesting that LLMs lose attention to security patterns as generation length increases.

\subsection{RQ6: Comparison with Human-Written Code}

To contextualize our findings, we compare LLM-generated contracts against 241 verified, deployed Ethereum mainnet contracts collected from Etherscan. These represent real-world human-written code from major protocols including Uniswap, Aave, Compound, Gnosis Safe, Lido, and others. All contracts were flattened using Foundry's \texttt{forge flatten} to resolve multi-file dependencies, with automatic compiler version detection via \texttt{solc-select}.

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{Comparison of LLM-generated vs.\ human-written contracts.}
\label{tab:human-baseline}
\centering

\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}

Three key observations emerge. First, human-written contracts exhibit a \emph{higher} absolute vulnerability count (mean 11.51 vs.\ 3.28) and a higher percentage of contracts with at least one finding (82.6\% vs.\ 64.5\%). This is primarily explained by contract complexity: human contracts average 446.7 LOC compared to 57.2 for LLM-generated code---a 7.8$\times$ difference. When normalized by lines of code, vulnerability density is comparable: 2.69 vulns/100 LOC for human contracts falls within the LLM range of 2.22--8.43.

Second, both datasets trigger the \emph{same} dominant detectors: \texttt{reentrancy-events}, \texttt{missing-zero-check}, \texttt{timestamp}, and \texttt{shadowing-local} appear in the top findings for both human and LLM code. This confirms that our tools measure consistent patterns across both populations and that many findings represent stylistic issues rather than exploitable vulnerabilities.

Third, the high-severity proportion is nearly identical (16.0\% human vs.\ 15.7\% LLM), suggesting that LLMs do not introduce fundamentally different vulnerability classes. However, the critical distinction is not in code quality but in the \emph{deployment pipeline}: human-written mainnet contracts have undergone professional auditing, testing, and monitoring, while LLM-generated contracts bypass these safeguards entirely. The danger of LLM-generated smart contracts lies not in their vulnerability density---which is comparable to human code---but in the security infrastructure they encourage developers to skip.

We note that the 54.8\% human baseline compilation rate introduces selection bias toward simpler contracts. To mitigate this, we performed a Semgrep-only comparison across all 241 human contracts (which does not require compilation), yielding consistent results: both populations trigger the same dominant detectors at similar rates.
