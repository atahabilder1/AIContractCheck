\begin{abstract}
Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly used by developers to generate Solidity smart contracts that manage billions of dollars in decentralized finance (DeFi). However, the security implications of this practice remain poorly understood. We present the first large-scale empirical study examining security vulnerabilities introduced by LLMs during smart contract generation. We generated 1,800 smart contracts across six state-of-the-art LLMs---GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite, DeepSeek Coder V2, Qwen 2.5 Coder 32B, and CodeLlama-34B---spanning 20 contract categories including high-impact cross-chain bridge and flash loan contracts. Using a multi-tool analysis pipeline combining Slither, Mythril, and Semgrep, we detected and categorized 5,007 vulnerabilities mapped to 10 distinct CWE identifiers. Our results reveal that 72.7\% of compilable LLM-generated contracts contain at least one security vulnerability, with reentrancy (CWE-841) and timestamp dependence (CWE-829) being the most prevalent. Contrary to expectations, adversarial prompts exploiting gas optimization and simplicity biases do \emph{not} increase vulnerability rates---instead, standard prompts produce significantly more vulnerabilities (mean 5.02 vs.\ 3.39, $p < 0.001$), suggesting that vulnerabilities are intrinsic to LLM code generation rather than prompt-dependent. We identify three LLM-specific vulnerability patterns---phantom guards, inherited vulnerabilities, and context window degradation---and find statistically significant differences across LLMs ($H = 138.8$, $p < 0.001$, Kruskal-Wallis test). Compilation rates vary dramatically, from 90.0\% (GPT-4o) to 34.7\% (CodeLlama), representing a critical and previously unreported quality dimension. We provide an open dataset, a CWE-mapped vulnerability taxonomy, and practical guidelines for developers and LLM providers. Our findings underscore the urgent need for LLM-aware security tooling in the smart contract development pipeline.
\end{abstract}
